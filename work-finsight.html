Finsight

Role UX Designer & Researcher
Tools Figma, Miro, MS Forms
Part of UX Tree Mentorship
Overview

FinSight is an app designed to help users manage their mortgage, with a focus on mortgage repayments. The project addresses a core user problem: homeowners often see their mortgage as a static, long-term burden. My vision was to empower homeowners to visualise and accelerate their mortgage journey, with the primary goal of making repayments more manageable.

User Problem

Homeowners see their mortgage as a static, long-term burden. They struggle with:

  • Lack of clear progress visibility
  • The feeling of long-term debt
  • Uncertainty about whether to pay extra, save, or invest
  • Difficulty understanding mortgage terms and impact

Approach

A visualization tool that shows how small financial actions today lead to significant savings tomorrow:

  • Personalized guidance based on individual finances
  • Visualized impact of repayments
  • Simplified decision-making with clear comparisons

User Research

Survey

I conducted an online survey targeting homeowners with a mortgage, which gathered 23 responses. The survey was distributed through social media, my friends and family network, the UX Tree community, and the finance forum boards.ie. After collecting the responses, I performed a thematic analysis on the data, which revealed four key themes. The main finding was that the core mortgage challenge is psychological, not just financial. Specifically, the top three struggles users face are the overwhelming feeling of long-term debt, difficulty understanding complex terms and conditions, and a distinct lack of visibility into their repayment progress.

Behaviour insights:

  • 6/23 participants have never calculated overpayments
  • 11/23 have never made an overpayment
  • Only 2/23 always make overpayments

Online Survey

Interviews

To gain deeper insights, I conducted six one-to-one interviews with homeowners who have a mortgage. Participants were recruited from the online survey. The methodology was thematic analysis.

Target Audience

My main user is William, a homeowner from Dublin. He feels stressed by his long-term mortgage and gets confused by his bank statements.William wants to feel secure and in control. He dreams of paying off his mortgage early, but he doesn’t want to miss out on saving for holidays or his future. He needs one simple, trustworthy tool that can show him his entire financial plan in one place, help him understand his options, and give him the confidence to make good decisions.
He represents the core user: motivated but overwhelmed, seeking control but unsure of the path forward.

Primary Persona

User Journey

Mapping William’s experience helped identify pain points and opportunities for intervention.

As-Is Journey

I created an as-is scenario that follows my primary persona as he tracks his budget and finances through manual spreadsheets. This journey is characterised by friction, guesswork, and periodic stress, leading to avoidance.

To-Be Journey

I then designed a to-be scenario where William sets up and manages his repayments through the FinSight app. This journey is streamlined, informed, and motivating, turning a monthly chore into an empowering step toward his goals.

Brainstorming

Storyboard

I created a visual storyboard to map out the key user journey and emotional experience for a homeowner like William. The storyboard illustrates his path from initial decision paralysis and stress over managing his mortgage to discovering an app that provides clear, personalized guidance. It visually narrates how connecting his accounts gives him a tangible vision of his financial future, empowering him to take action and ultimately feel in control and confident as a homeowner.

Design & Prototyping

Design Considerations

Before moving into prototyping, I defined the core principles that would guide every design decision.

  • Empowering Language: Use positive, action-oriented copy that focuses on progress and control.
  • Clear Path & Impact: Ensure every screen shows users where they are, what they can do, and what the outcome will be.
  • Personalised Insights: Provide recommendations and data based on the user’s unique financial situation.
  • Progress Visualisation: Make abstract financial progress tangible and visually rewarding.
  • Human-Centred Design: Prioritise emotional needs (reducing anxiety, building confidence) alongside functional requirements.

Conclusion

Limitations

  • Learning Curve: The app deals with complex financial concepts, requiring careful onboarding to avoid overwhelming new users.
  • Lack of Direct Competitors: While a gap in the market, it also meant fewer existing benchmarks for feature sets and user expectations.
  • Complexity of Features: Integrating bank account data, calculating complex mortgage scenarios, and presenting them simply was a significant design and technical challenge.

Main Learnings

  • Knowledge Bias: I initially overestimated users’ financial literacy, leading to early designs that used too much jargon. Testing was crucial to identify and correct this.
  • Using Visuals to Convey Meaning: A well-designed chart or timeline can communicate complex financial impact more effectively than paragraphs of text or tables of numbers.
  • Feature Prioritisation Early in the Process: Focusing on the most importnat features first.
